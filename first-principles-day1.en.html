Personal Finance Day 1

First Principles of Personal Finance

Before any talk of "making money grow," get the foundation right—why a high income isn't the same as being wealthy, and the one number that decides everything.

个人理财 · 理财第一性原理　|　2026-07-14

Most personal-finance content jumps straight to what to buy and where to invest. But here's the uncomfortable truth: whether you eventually reach financial independence is decided mainly not by how much you earn, nor by your investment returns, but by one number you can almost fully control—your savings rate. High earners with negative net worth, and ordinary earners who bank seven figures, are both common. This issue gives you no investment picks—just four foundations: net worth is the truth, savings rate sets your timeline, compounding also works on the spending side, and how to run a health check using a few ratios. Get the foundation right and every later step gets easier.

Four Foundations

POINT 1

Net Worth, Not Income, Is Wealth

How much you have is your net worth (assets − liabilities), not your paycheck.

Mechanism. Income is a flow; net worth is a stock. A high income only becomes wealth when it "stays." The Millionaire Next Door (Stanley & Danko) found that many high earners with luxury cars and big houses have low net worth (the book calls them "under-accumulators of wealth"), while plenty of low-key, ordinary earners are the real wealthy ones. Wealth is the part you don't see—money not spent, saved, and invested.

Actionable. Use the book's rough formula to locate yourself:

Expected net worth ≈ age × pretax annual income ÷ 10

Calibration warning: income-anchored rules of thumb like this systematically overstate for high earners / high marginal-tax brackets / equity-comp-heavy incomes—(1) pretax income overstates the money you actually keep (tax bites super-linearly as income rises); (2) "age × income" assumes you earned today's income your whole life, whereas high pay usually only ramps up in your 30s. The higher your income, the more you should switch to a harder yardstick: net worth ÷ annual spending (see Points 2 & 4). Don't treat this formula as a target.

Common myth: "My salary is high, so I'm rich." — High salary + high spending + zero saving can leave net worth negative (student loans / car loan / credit cards). Income gives you potential, not wealth.
This week: Compute your current net worth (all assets − all liabilities) and write it down—it's the origin point of everything. Reflection: if the paycheck stopped tomorrow, how long would that net worth support you?
POINT 2

Your Savings Rate Sets the Timeline

How soon you reach financial independence is set mainly by your savings rate, not income or returns.

Mechanism. Savings rate = (income − spending) / income. Here "saving" means income you don't spend and then invest—not money parked in a bank. It works from both ends at once: save more → assets grow faster; spend less → the target you need is smaller too. That double action makes it far more powerful than investment returns. A widely cited estimate: assuming a 5% long-run real return, a 4% withdrawal rate, and starting from zero, savings rate alone roughly decides how many years you must work.

Savings rateApprox. years to financial independence
10%~51 yrs
20%~37 yrs
30%~28 yrs
50%~17 yrs
65%~10.5 yrs

This is a model, not a promise: returns fluctuate and life changes. But the trend is extremely robust—moving your savings rate from 10% to 30% cuts off more than twenty years.

Common myth: "Once I get a raise / find a great investment, I'll be free." — If a raise is eaten by proportional spending (lifestyle inflation), your savings rate is unchanged and the timeline doesn't move a day.
This week: Compute your real savings rate for last month. Reflection: to raise it by 5 points, which cut would hurt the least?
POINT 3

Compounding Cuts Both Ways

Every recurring expense is reverse-compounding your future away.

Mechanism. Everyone knows about investment compounding. But spending compounds too: a recurring monthly expense doesn't just cost that money—it costs its opportunity cost of compounding for decades; and it raises your "baseline cost of living," which raises the target you need for financial independence. By the 4% rule, spending $10k more per year → your FI target must be $250k larger. So cutting a recurring expense is a double win: it instantly raises your savings rate and permanently lowers your target.

Actionable. Distinguish one-time from recurring spending. Subscriptions, memberships, fixed monthly bills deserve the hardest look—they compound in the background. Reframe "should I buy this" as: am I willing to permanently raise my FI target by 25× this amount?

Common myth: "It's just a coffee a month." — The latte factor is overhyped, but the direction is right: what's overlooked is the recurring, not the single amount. A $50/month subscription = FI target +$15k.
This week: List every recurring subscription and cut one you didn't use last month. Reflection: which spending truly raises your happiness, and which is just habit or keeping up?
POINT 4

Run a Health Check: Ratios, Not Balances

Financial health is a few ratios, not the absolute number in your bank account.

Mechanism. Balances mislead—someone with $500k saved but $800k of debt is less healthy than someone with $50k saved and no debt. A real check looks at a few ratios:

  • Savings rate: (income − spending) / income — decides the future
  • Emergency buffer: liquid assets ÷ monthly essential spending — target 3–6 months
  • Debt ratio: total liabilities ÷ total assets — lower is steadier
  • Liquidity: money you can use immediately ÷ monthly spending

Fill these four in each quarter (or year) and watch the trend, like a checkup—rather than fixating on some day's balance with anxiety or false comfort.

Common myth: "The number in my account is big, so I'm fine." — Big balance + big debt + zero buffer = one layoff away from collapse. Absolute numbers soothe and numb; ratios tell the truth.
This week: Compute these four ratios and build a table (next issue covers how to automate the whole system). Reflection: which ratio is your weakest, and what habit does it expose?

Note for High-Earning Tech Workers

One per issue, focused on the specific situation of this group: equity comp / heavy taxes / volatile income.

  • Your main lever is the spending side, not earning more. A high income makes a high savings rate physically possible, but peer spending, big-city costs, and the "sudden-wealth" feeling of vesting equity quietly raise your spending baseline. Protecting your savings rate comes from resisting lifestyle inflation, not from earning still more.
  • Don't count unvested / employer-concentrated equity as net worth you can use. When computing usable net worth, itemize unvested equity and the slice heavily concentrated in your own employer separately—your human capital (salary) is already tied to that company; investing there again is double concentration.
  • Run everything on an after-tax basis. In high marginal brackets, pretax figures badly overstate your real ability to accumulate; compute savings rate, net worth, and the FI target on the money you keep, not the number on the offer.

Deeper Questions

Is savings rate the master variable for everyone?
No. When income is near subsistence, rent and food are rigid and the numerator barely moves—here "raise your savings rate" is the wrong main line; "raise income / skills / relocate" is. Savings rate is the master variable for middle-to-high earners; the relative power of the two levers (earn more vs. spend less) depends on where you sit on the income curve. Honestly: this issue's framework is most useful for those who already have a surplus to allocate.
Can "maximizing net worth" become its own disease?
Yes. Living your life as a balance sheet, sacrificing present experiences and relationships for a number, is backwards. Wealth is a means, not an end—the highest "dividend" it can buy is control over your own time. Build the foundation, but net worth serves freedom, not the reverse. "Enough" is itself a skill worth practicing; a later issue covers it.
What special traps do high-earning tech workers face?
A few: high income makes it easy to think "no need to do the math"; vesting equity creates large year-to-year income swings plus single-stock concentration; an environment where peers all spend (the neighbor effect). Conversely, a high savings rate is especially powerful for high earners—at the same 50% rate, both the absolute accumulation and the time compression are more extreme. Equity comp (RSU/ISO/ESPP) and its tax landmines get dedicated issues later in this series.
